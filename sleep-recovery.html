Sleep, Rest & Recovery

Stronger swings are built in training — improved in recovery

A player does not become elite by swinging until tired every day. A player becomes elite by combining quality reps, smart cage work, daily wrist training, explosive movement, good food, enough sleep, and recovery days.

Why sleep matters for baseball

Sleep supports reaction time, coordination, focus, muscle recovery, skill memory, energy, mood, and confidence. Baseball is a skill sport — if a player is tired, the swing gets sloppy, timing gets late, and confidence drops.

Kemuel Recovery Rule

Train with structure, not exhaustion

Don't confuse soreness with progress. If a player feels pain, dizziness, unusual fatigue, numbness, tingling, or sharp discomfort — stop training and tell a parent or coach.

Starter Weekly Recovery Standard

Build without burning out

Starter players should not exceed 4 total cage/BP visits per week.

  • 1 pitching-machine session
  • 1 batting practice session
  • 2 box jump sessions
  • Daily light gyroball work
  • At least 1 full recovery day

    • Recovery day activities

    Light walking, gentle stretching, easy mobility, light gyroball control, swing video review, journaling, hydration, early bedtime.

    Avoid on recovery days

    Max-effort swings, heavy box jumps, hard throwing, long cage sessions, and training through pain.

    Parent note: the goal is not a tired kid — it's a stronger, more confident, better-prepared baseball player. Recovery is part of the program.
